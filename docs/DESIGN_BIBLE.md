# Redemption's Edge Design Bible

Working title: Redemption's Edge
First vertical slice location: Hicksville / Mercy Ridge concept
Genre: 2D open-world western arcade action adventure
Camera: Top-down / three-quarter top-down
Core references: classic open-world Zelda structure, outlaw sandbox chaos, hand-drawn cartoon western presentation
Player character: Chris King, aka "Dckslinger"
Project direction: grow from the current single-file HTML prototype into a larger multi-file browser game project
Camp leader: Darryl
Starting motivation: redemption and freedom
First supernatural threat: desert demons
First major antagonist: Silas Voss
First Hollowed visual: bone-faced gunslingers
Chris's central wound: the Ash Hollow Massacre

## 1. High Concept

Redemption's Edge is a chaotic, heroic, funny western outlaw adventure set in a haunted desert frontier where the player can become a legend, a menace, or something messier in between.

The game should feel like a Saturday-morning outlaw myth filtered through a dusty 1930s cartoon reel: rugged characters, dramatic showdowns, weird humor, crooked towns, haunted landmarks, exaggerated villains, and fast arcade gunfights.

The player is Chris King, aka "Dckslinger," a true outlaw. They can help people, rob people, charm townsfolk, become wanted, cause GTA-style trouble, or stumble into redemption after doing things the hard way.

## 2. Creative Pillars

### Heroic Chaos

The player should constantly feel powerful, reckless, and slightly out of control. Fights should have smoke, ricochets, panic, dodges, explosions, charging enemies, desperate reloads, and slapstick western consequences.

### Outlaw Freedom

The player can be bad if they want. Robbing, fighting, stealing, threatening, and causing trouble should be possible. The world reacts with wanted pressure, bounty hunters, price changes, fear, gossip, and locked-out opportunities.

### Cartoon Drama

The visuals can be exaggerated and funny, but the characters should still feel rugged and dramatic. The tone is not pure parody. It is an outlaw cartoon with real stakes.

### Dense Open World

The first region should be small but rich: secrets, shortcuts, odd strangers, caves, hidden loot, locked doors, ambushes, bounty posters, environmental jokes, and optional trouble.

### Arcade Combat First

Movement, shooting, dodging, lassoing, explosions, enemy waves, gang fights, and boss encounters matter more than slow realism. The game should feel instantly fun before the story asks for patience.

## 3. Tone

Primary tone:
- Heroic
- Chaotic
- Funny
- Rugged
- Dramatic
- Dark

Humor style:
- Old-cartoon exaggeration
- Visual gags
- Big personality NPCs
- Weird frontier superstition
- Absurd consequences for player chaos
- Very dark comedy, with the world allowed to get nasty, violent, strange, and morally wrong

Drama style:
- Outlaws with loyalty problems
- Haunted places where the supernatural is real
- Towns hiding corruption
- Moral choices that do not stop the player from being wild
- A protagonist chasing redemption and freedom while being fully capable of making everything worse

## 4. World Premise

The first region is a haunted desert outlaw territory built around Hicksville, a crooked frontier town that survives by pretending not to notice the crimes paying its bills.

Working naming direction:
Mercy Ridge is the town's respectable official name. Hicksville can be the nickname, old name, insult, or local outlaw name for the same place. This gives the town two faces: the polished cattle-stop lie and the rotten joke everyone knows underneath.

The supernatural is real in this world. Ghosts, cursed places, impossible desert creatures, haunted objects, bone shrines, strange lights, and old frontier legends can all become actual gameplay and story material.

The first supernatural threat is desert demons. They should feel like something the frontier has buried, denied, and profited from. Hicksville treats them like a joke, rumor, scam, curse, and business opportunity all at once, until Chris finds out they are real.

### The Town's Big Lie

The town appears prosperous from a distance: cattle arrive daily, saloons stay full, merchants are wealthy, and travelers are encouraged to stop before crossing the surrounding wasteland.

But people disappear after dark. Livestock is torn apart without tracks. Travelers hear familiar voices calling from the dust. Human-shaped figures move beyond the fences at night.

The town does not call them demons. Officially, the deaths are animal attacks, bandit raids, heat sickness, drunken wandering, or bad luck. Everyone knows the truth, but the town's economy depends on keeping the truth hidden.

The town profits from the disappearances:
- Abandoned cattle are resold
- Missing travelers' belongings are auctioned
- Hotels keep charging for rooms no one will return to
- Fake bounties are placed on "bandits" who do not exist
- Ranchers collect payments on livestock losses
- Desperate outsiders are quietly steered toward unsafe routes

The town is not simply afraid of the demons. It has learned how to make money from them.

### The Hollowed

The first demon type is known in old records as the Hollowed.

Behavior:
- Appear mostly at night or during dust storms
- Resemble dehydrated travelers, wounded ranch hands, lost children, or people from the victim's memory
- Imitate voices and regrets
- Hunt people carrying guilt, grief, secrets, or buried violence
- Become especially interested in Chris King because of his past

The Hollowed are not just monsters to shoot. They should attack Chris's identity. At first, Chris thinks the visions are heat, alcohol, exhaustion, or guilt. Eventually, he realizes they know things no living person should know.

Their first recurring accusation:
"You said you were coming back."

### Boundary Ritual

The town survives because an old ritual beneath the abandoned church keeps the Hollowed from entering the center of town.

The protection has a price: the Hollowed must be allowed to hunt beyond the marked boundary. Town leadership uses that arrangement to protect the rich center while feeding strangers, criminals, drifters, prisoners, and unwanted residents to the desert.

This creates the first chapter's central question:
How many innocent people can a community sacrifice before survival becomes another form of evil?

Hicksville sits between:
- A dusty town center with shops, sheriff pressure, wanted posters, and secrets behind locked doors
- A haunted desert wilderness with bones, mirages, abandoned shrines, and strange night encounters
- An outlaw camp where the player can upgrade, regroup, and pick up story hooks
- A cave/dungeon with treasure, enemies, hazards, and a boss clue
- A rival gang hideout that acts as the vertical slice's main enemy stronghold

## 5. Player Fantasy

The player is a true outlaw with the freedom to choose:
- Folk hero
- Charming rogue
- Chaotic criminal
- Feared menace
- Accidental redeemer

The game should never force the player to behave. It should let them act, then make the world push back.

Player identity:
- Name: Chris King
- Alias: Dckslinger
- Starting role: outlaw already tied to a camp
- Moral freedom: civilians are killable and robbable, with strong consequences
- Starting motivation: redemption and freedom

### Chris's Past: The Ash Hollow Massacre

Seven years before the game begins, Chris worked as an armed enforcer for a powerful cattle and land company. The company wanted Ash Hollow, a small desert settlement built around one of the region's few dependable water sources. The residents refused to sell, so the company hired gunmen to frighten them into leaving.

Darryl led the operation.

Chris was told the residents were thieves, squatters, and cattle rustlers. His job was to seize weapons, destroy stored supplies, and force everyone onto company wagons.

Then the operation turned into a massacre.

Someone fired first. Nobody ever agreed who. Darryl's men set fire to the settlement and shot people trying to escape. Many residents fled into an abandoned silver mine beneath the town. Chris followed them inside and found families, wounded residents, surrendered men, and children hiding in the tunnels.

One woman saw Chris hesitate and begged him to lead them through another exit. Chris promised he would return for them.

Instead, Darryl ordered the mine sealed. Darryl claimed armed residents were preparing an ambush and warned Chris that letting them escape would expose everyone involved.

Chris knew Darryl was lying. He also knew refusing could get him killed.

Chris helped place the explosives.

Before the mine entrance collapsed, he heard the people inside pounding against the supports and calling his name.

The company blamed bandits. Chris accepted payment. For years, he told himself he had no choice, that they were already doomed, and that Darryl would have sealed the mine without him.

But Chris knows the truth: he made them a promise, then buried them alive.

### Ash Hollow and the Hollowed

Ash Hollow was built over an ancient burial chamber. The people hiding in the mine accidentally crossed into it while searching for another exit. Their fear, betrayal, and slow deaths awakened the beings later known as the Hollowed.

The Hollowed are not literally the ghosts of Chris's victims, but they absorbed their final memories:
- The pounding on the mine entrance
- Chris's promise to return
- His footsteps walking away
- The company lie that erased them

This is why the Hollowed know Chris's name.

To the Hollowed, Chris is:
- A witness: he carries the truth the company erased
- A doorway: his guilt lets them reach into dreams, visions, and places they normally could not enter
- A possible vessel: if he gives in to denial, rage, or hopelessness, they may be able to enter the physical world through him

They do not immediately want to kill Chris. They want him to remember.

### Redemption Definition

Chris cannot redeem himself by simply killing monsters or outlaws. His first real act of redemption is telling the truth about Ash Hollow even though the truth condemns him.

The central wound of Chris's character:
He promised to return for people who trusted him.

His redemption means becoming the kind of man who finally goes back.

## 6. First Region: Hicksville and the Haunted Flats

### Hicksville

Hicksville is the first town. It should be memorable, strange, and immediately readable.

Tone mix:
Hicksville is spooky, funny, corrupt, and pathetic all at once. It should look like a town pretending to be respectable while every wall, cellar, and alley says otherwise.

If Mercy Ridge becomes the official name, Hicksville should be what outlaws, locals, and bitter travelers call it once they see the truth.

Possible landmarks:
- The Leaning Saloon
- Sheriff Crook's Office
- The Lucky Tooth General Store
- Undertaker and Taxidermy
- The Broken Spur Stable
- Hicksville Bank
- Old Well
- Abandoned chapel
- Back-alley lockpick doors
- Wanted board

Town gameplay:
- Buy ammo, tonics, dynamite, tools
- Rob townsfolk and shops
- Trigger sheriff/law response
- Pick up bounties
- Hear rumors that point to secrets
- Discover locked buildings and hidden basements
- Start or advance the three vertical-slice missions

### Haunted Desert Wilderness

The wilderness should be compact but dense.

Features:
- Cactus fields
- Dry riverbed
- Bone arch
- Ghost lantern trail
- Abandoned wagon circle
- Collapsed mine entrance
- Strange rock formations
- Desert shrine
- Hidden treasure dig spots
- Ambush zones

Gameplay:
- Explore freely
- Find secrets
- Fight roaming bandits
- Use tools to access new areas
- Trigger random encounters
- Collect money, ammo, fish, relics, and upgrade materials

### Outlaw Camp

The player starts with an outlaw camp. The camp is run by Darryl and acts as the player's home base, social hub, upgrade station, and story anchor.

Functions:
- Rest and heal
- Upgrade tools and weapons
- Change outfit later
- Store horse
- Talk to companion NPCs
- Start story missions
- Donate money or stolen goods
- Hear rumors

Camp should feel funny and alive, not like a menu.

The camp should exist from the beginning, not as a later unlock. It gives the player somewhere to belong before Hicksville starts judging them.

### Darryl

Darryl runs the camp. He should be a memorable anchor character: part shady uncle, part ex-preacher, practical, funny, morally flexible, and somehow still more responsible than everyone else around him.

Darryl was an old outlaw partner of Chris's family and preached in the church Chris grew up in. That makes him family-adjacent: not necessarily blood, but close enough that his approval, disappointment, and betrayal all matter.

Design role:
- Gives the camp identity
- Offers main mission direction
- Comments on Chris King's chaos
- Helps frame the redemption/freedom theme
- Acts as the first stable relationship in the game

Possible personality direction:
Darryl has seen enough frontier stupidity to expect the worst from everyone, but he still believes the camp can survive if Chris stops turning every errand into a public incident. His ex-preacher side gives him a talent for speeches, guilt, scripture-adjacent warnings, and dark jokes about salvation. His shady-uncle side means he will absolutely lie, steal, and manipulate if it keeps the camp breathing.

Dark secret:
Darryl led the Ash Hollow operation and knows exactly why the Hollowed recognize Chris. After Ash Hollow, Darryl studied the creatures and learned how their hunger could be redirected. This knowledge eventually connects to the boundary ritual protecting Mercy Ridge.

This should make Darryl complicated, not disposable. He is camp leader, mentor, old family connection, preacher-shaped manipulator, and part of Chris's original wound.

Important adaptation note:
Darryl should not also be the first town's main villain. Keeping Darryl as the camp leader gives the player a stable home-base relationship. The town's corrupt power broker should be a separate character, which lets Darryl comment on the situation, know more than he admits, or have a past connection without becoming the boss himself.

### Town Power Broker

Working antagonist role:
A cattle boss, unofficial mayor, and financier of the sheriff's office controls Mercy Ridge/Hicksville.

Placeholder name: Silas Voss.
Locked name: Silas Voss.

Public face:
- Owns the largest ranch
- Controls the stockyards
- Finances the sheriff
- Takes a percentage from nearly every business
- Denies the Hollowed exist
- Offers Chris work, money, protection, and a clean way out
- Has an unforgettable comic silhouette: a huge cartoon backside that makes him visually absurd before he becomes threatening

Private truth:
- Understands the boundary ritual
- Sends unwanted people beyond the markers
- Treats the arrangement as business, not worship
- Believes survival excuses cruelty

Possible line:
"Every town is built on somebody's bones. We're just more honest about where ours are buried."

Visual direction:
Silas Voss should be both ridiculous and intimidating. His oversized cartoon butt can be part of his silhouette, swagger, and animation language: chair creaks, belt strains, dust puffs when he sits, and pompous waddling authority. The joke should make him memorable, but his choices should still feel cruel and dangerous.

## 7. Gameplay Style

The game should be open-world in the classic Zelda sense:
- Explore a readable overworld
- Follow roads or wander off-road
- Find secrets without being told
- Unlock new tools
- Return to earlier places with new abilities
- Clear dangerous locations
- Meet odd NPCs
- Fight bosses
- Build mastery through movement and combat

This does not mean heavy dungeon puzzles as the main attraction. Puzzles should be light, fast, and physical.

## 8. Core Loop

1. Leave camp or town.
2. Explore the region.
3. Get into trouble, fight, rob, help, or investigate.
4. Earn money, ammo, tools, reputation, or wanted heat.
5. Find secrets, missions, bounties, or locked areas.
6. Return to camp/town to heal, upgrade, buy, or trigger the next conflict.
7. Push deeper into the region until the boss/gang fight becomes available.

## 9. Combat Direction

Combat should become more arcade-like than the current prototype.

Priority changes:
- Faster player movement
- Dodge/dash with brief invulnerability or damage reduction
- More enemies on screen
- Clear attack tells
- Stronger knockback and hit reactions
- Explosive dynamite
- Ricochet or trick-shot opportunities
- More generous pickups
- Boss/gang fights with phases
- Dead Eye as a dramatic power moment

### Player Combat Kit

Initial or unlockable tools:
- Revolver
- Lasso
- Dynamite
- Fishing pole
- Horse whistle
- Bow
- Lockpick
- Dead Eye ability

Recommended unlock order for vertical slice:
1. Revolver and dash: available immediately
2. Lasso: early mission reward
3. Dynamite: bought or stolen before the cave
4. Lockpick: lets players access town secrets
5. Horse whistle: unlock after first outlaw camp mission
6. Bow: optional stealth/hunting tool
7. Fishing pole: side activity and economy tool
8. Dead Eye: unlocked before the boss fight

## 10. Tool Design

### Revolver

The default weapon. Fast, punchy, limited ammo cylinder, quick manual reload.

Design goals:
- Snappy shooting
- Strong muzzle flash
- Clear ammo rhythm
- Arcade recoil, not sluggish realism

### Dash / Dodge

Core movement upgrade. Should make combat feel alive.

Uses:
- Avoid bullets
- Cross gaps or hazards later
- Escape surrounded fights
- Create skill expression

### Lasso

A western utility tool.

Uses:
- Pull small enemies
- Stun bandits
- Grab loot from range
- Pull switches/levers
- Catch animals
- Start funny NPC interactions

### Dynamite

The chaos tool.

Uses:
- Blow up rocks
- Damage groups
- Open cave paths
- Trigger environmental gags
- Increase wanted level if used in town

### Lockpick

Town secret tool.

Uses:
- Open back rooms
- Rob locked chests
- Find hidden routes
- Break into suspicious buildings

### Horse Whistle

Traversal and identity tool.

Uses:
- Summon horse
- Cross wilderness faster
- Escape wanted pressure
- Trigger horse-based comedy moments

### Bow

Quiet alternative weapon.

Uses:
- Hunting
- Stealth kills
- Silent puzzle shots
- Fire arrows later

### Fishing Pole

Side loop and utility item.

Uses:
- Fishing economy
- Pull floating items
- Hook distant switches
- Catch strange haunted desert fish in impossible places

### Dead Eye

Signature power.

Uses:
- Slow time
- Mark enemies
- Fire a stylish burst
- Turn boss chaos into a dramatic skill moment

## 11. Morality and Chaos Systems

The game should allow genuinely bad behavior.

### Wanted Level

Short-term pressure.

Raised by:
- Shooting in town
- Robbing shops
- Hurting townsfolk
- Killing townsfolk
- Blowing things up
- Stealing horses
- Ignoring sheriff warnings

Effects:
- Lawmen spawn
- Shops close
- Townsfolk flee
- Bounty hunters track the player in wilderness
- Roadblocks appear
- Boss-like sheriff response at high levels

### Honor / Reputation

Long-term identity.

Possible range:
- Feared outlaw
- Neutral drifter
- Folk hero

Effects:
- Dialogue changes
- Prices change
- Certain NPCs help or refuse
- Endings or mission outcomes shift later
- Some companions approve/disapprove

Important: honor should react to chaos but should not prevent chaos.

Civilian rule:
Townsfolk are killable and robbable. This should create strong wanted and reputation consequences, but the game should not hard-block the player from doing it. The fun comes from freedom plus consequences.

## 12. Visual Direction

The target is a blended hand-drawn look inspired by old cartoons and rugged western illustration.

Key traits:
- Thick ink outlines
- Exaggerated silhouettes
- Hand-painted backgrounds
- Bouncy animation
- Dust, smoke, impact stars, speed lines
- Film grain and slight vignette
- Dramatic western color palettes
- More rugged than cute

Cuphead-like inspiration should come from craft, not copying:
- Hand-drawn animation feel
- Painted backgrounds
- Old cartoon motion principles
- Strong character silhouettes
- Musical rhythm and visual timing

Avoid:
- Directly copying Cuphead characters, UI, bosses, or exact visual motifs
- Directly copying Zelda names, icons, maps, characters, or dungeons
- Directly copying Red Dead names, characters, or exact story beats

## 13. Graphics / Rendering Approach

Current prototype: single-file HTML5 Canvas with procedural shapes.

Long-term project direction:
Move toward a larger multi-file browser game project instead of keeping everything in one HTML file forever. The single-file prototype is useful for quick sharing, but the real game should become modular as systems grow.

Near-term visual upgrade:
- Keep Canvas 2D
- Add a sprite/animation system
- Use hand-drawn-style placeholder sprites
- Add parallax background layers
- Add screen-space overlays: film grain, dust, vignette
- Add squash/stretch animation states
- Add impact frames and smear effects

Later visual pipeline:
- Character sprite sheets
- Background tiles or painted map chunks
- Prop sprite atlas
- Animated effect atlas
- Boss sprite sheets
- UI frames and title cards

Technical goal:
The renderer should support a hand-drawn cartoon look while staying playable in a browser.

Recommended future structure:
- `index.html`: page shell and canvas
- `src/main.js`: bootstrapping and game loop
- `src/core/`: input, camera, save/load, audio, collision
- `src/entities/`: player, enemies, NPCs, horse, bullets, pickups
- `src/systems/`: wanted, honor, missions, dialogue, inventory, tools
- `src/world/`: maps, regions, buildings, encounters, secrets
- `src/rendering/`: sprite animation, particles, effects, UI
- `assets/`: sprite sheets, concept art, audio, fonts, UI frames
- `docs/`: design bible, roadmap, mission specs

- A staged argument turns into a shootout.
- Player can help, rob during the chaos, or flee.
- Sheriff reacts based on player behavior.
- Ends with a lead pointing to missing travelers, false bandit bounties, and the town's boundary markers.

- Wanted level

- Player investigates a dry riverbed wagon ambush.

## 16. First Boss Encounter

Working boss direction: Silas Voss, his gunmen, and the Hollowed

Tone:
Funny, mean, dramatic, and visually loud.

Arena:
The confrontation begins during a cattle auction as a dust storm rolls in, then moves through stockyards, town streets, the abandoned church, and the ritual chamber below.

Phases:
1. Chris escapes jail, a holding barn, or a setup by the sheriff's office
2. Stockyard chase while panicked cattle break through fences
3. Gunfight against Voss's men as Hollowed move through the dust
4. Descent into the abandoned church's ritual chamber
5. Boss confrontation where Voss tries to preserve the boundary at any cost
6. Dead Eye finish window or ritual decision

Rewards:
- Region cleared
- Camp upgrade money
- Reputation shift
- New map clue for the next region

### First Chapter Choice

Best-practice implementation:
The vertical slice should introduce and lightly prototype the choice, but the full three-outcome consequence system should be saved for the fuller Chapter One build. For the first playable slice, the player can make a contained end-of-mission decision with immediate feedback, reputation change, and a teaser ending. The larger world-state consequences should come later, after the town, wanted system, missions, and Hollowed mechanics are stronger.

The full boss finale should eventually support three possible outcomes:

Preserve the boundary:
Chris prevents the immediate sacrifice but becomes marked by the Hollowed. The town survives temporarily, but the corrupt system may remain unless exposed.

Destroy the boundary:
Chris ends the agreement. The town must evacuate or fight for survival. The economy collapses, but future victims are not quietly fed to the desert.

Alter the agreement:
If the player investigates thoroughly, Chris can turn the ritual into a trap. The Hollowed are drawn into the underground chamber and sealed, but Chris must confront manifestations of people he harmed to complete it. This is the hardest and most redemptive outcome.

Ash Hollow connection:
The full choice should eventually force Chris to deal with the truth of Ash Hollow, not just the present-day Mercy Ridge ritual.
- Does Chris reveal his role in the massacre?
- Does he protect himself by hiding the truth?
- Does Darryl help, lie, or try to control the outcome?
- Do the Hollowed force Chris to hear the voices from the mine?
- Does redemption mean saving Mercy Ridge, condemning it, or finally telling the truth?
